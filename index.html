Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity homes built in North Central Connecticut

by Emma Willins and Myrian Ayala

Last updated on 12/04/2021

for Data Visualization for All
with Prof. Jack Dougherty
Trinity College, Hartford CT, USA

Story


Since 1989, Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity has been building affordable homes in the city of Hartford and outlying areas. One challenge the organization continually faces is finding land they can purchase and obtain permission to build affordable homes, since some suburban towns have resisted housing for low-income residents.Therefore, we wanted to answer the following question: Where has Habitat built homes in North Central Connecticut over time?. In order to do so, we were required to collect and visualize data illustrating the year that each of the 249 homes that were built as well as placing them in the context of Habitat’s expansion within and surrounding Hartford and outlying towns over time. Through collaboration with our Habitat for Humanity partners, they have shared their goal of expanding their reach beyond Hartford, providing their services outside of Connecticut’s capital.

Keeping Habitat’s goal in mind, we have made two charts that provide both a summary that speaks more directly to Habitat’s expansion in North Central Connecticut over the decade as well as Habitat’s data organized by location and corresponding decade of construction.

The following column bar chart examines the percentages of where Habitat built homes by decade. Although we can observe that this chart demonstrates that the majority of Habitat homes built reside in Hartford, we can also see that the percentage of Habitat homes in outlying towns has slowly increased over time.

Now, to see where exactly Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity has built homes in outlying towns outside of Hartford, we decided to create a bar chart which visually illustrates the proportion of Habitat homes among all other towns. Here the distinctions between outlying towns become clearer. New Britain stands out as the town that Habitat has done the most work in, which makes sense, given New Britain’s geographic proximity to Hartford. Additionally, New Britain has the largest population out of all the towns Habitat provided data for, excluding Hartford. It is reasonable that Habitat would pursue construction in larger towns given their work in more-population-dense areas such as Hartford.

Figure 2: Numbers of homes built by Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity in outlying areas outside Hartford's city per decade. Stacked bar chart created with Datawrapper. Explore the interactive version

To have a better understanding of where the homes built by Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity are located, we decided to create an interactive choropleth map for each decade that displays the name and the estimated number of homes built in each town. We decided to use a continuous linear color legend where the dark blue represents the greater amount of homes built while the lighter color refers to a lower number of homes built. For instance, in this case, the dark blue represents the city of Hartford since most of the total homes built by decade resides in Connecticut’s capital and the lighter colors are the outlying areas outside of the city where Habitat has built. We created our own geojson map template that combines Hartford and Tolland counties where most of the towns are located. However, only a huge part of the maps are not colored, which means that Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity has not built any home yet and is represented by the color gray.
